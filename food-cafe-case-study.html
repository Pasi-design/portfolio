Overview

Where the brief started — and where it actually went

QuickBite already had solid restaurant partnerships and steady order volume — the problem was everything in between. Customers stalled out browsing menus, customising orders and paying, all crammed onto screens trying to do too much at once. Restaurant partners, on the other side, were misreading order customisations almost as often as they filled them correctly. The brief became a focused two-week sprint: find where the friction actually was, then fix the handful of screens responsible for most of it.

Problem

Research and support patterns pointed to the same three issues

01

Crowded ordering screens — menu browsing, customisation and checkout all competed for the same screen, slowing every order down.

02

Thin payment options — a narrow set of payment methods pushed price-sensitive customers to abandon their cart before paying.

03

Misread orders — customisation notes reached restaurant partners as plain text, easy to misread under rush-hour pressure.

Process

Four stages, packed into two weeks

01 — Research

Talk to both sides

Surveys and interviews with frequent customers and restaurant partners surfaced where each group actually got stuck.

02 — Define

Map it visually

An empathy map and affinity diagram grouped the findings into a short list of problems worth solving first.

03 — Design

Sketch the real flows

Personas and user flows guided new wireframes for browsing, customising and checkout before any visual polish.

04 — Test

Watch people order

A moderated usability study on the new flow shaped the final design system and interface.

Solution

Three changes did most of the work

01

Ordering split into clear steps

Browsing, customising and paying became three distinct steps instead of one crowded screen, with a one-tap reorder shortcut for regulars.

02

More ways to pay

Added several payment methods and digital wallets, so customers were no longer stuck at checkout with one inconvenient option.

03

Order summaries partners can't misread

Customisation details now reach the restaurant side as a structured, itemised summary instead of free text — no more guessing what a scribbled note meant.

Result

What changed after testing

0Drop in checkout abandonment after adding more payment options
0Average usability score in testing, up from 3.3
0Increase in repeat orders within the first month
